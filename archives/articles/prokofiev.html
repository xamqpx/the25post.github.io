PROKOFIEV'S
GRIEF

By AMITAI SEGEV

25 January 2025

Sergei Prokofiev.

I’ll admit, as a fan of classical music, I have always been discouraged by Sergei Prokofiev. I have tried listening to his sonatas, symphonies, and quartets; but all I could ever remember him for was Romeo and Juliet. Part of this uneasiness purely lies in aesthetic preferences. He was not as Romantic as his contemporaries Franz Liszt or Frédéric Chopin, instead venturing into modernism. For that reason, I thought his music was simply not pleasant enough to listen to.

That is until I stumbled across Prokofiev’s Piano Concerto No. 2.

Written in 1912, Prokofiev had dedicated the piece to a close friend, Maximilian Schmidthof, who had taken his life after writing a farewell letter to the composer. With a short runtime of 30 minutes, Prokofiev created one of the most influential pieces for the piano — a grand testament to Prokofiev’s struggle with personal loss as well as the political turmoil of his home country, which was in the throes of the Russian Revolution — and set the precedent for his career as a composer who pushes boundaries melodically and physically.

I had never heard such abstract melodies that develop and evolve so beautifully in unimaginable ways before. On my first listen, part of me felt uncomfortable with the constant trembling of sounds that I thought would be impossible to play on the piano. Indeed it remains one of the most technically challenging pieces for the instrument to date. “A decade ago I’d have bet you there were only a dozen pianists in the world who could play Prokofiev’s Second Piano Concerto properly,” muses a 2012 BBC Music Magazine article. "[Martha] Argerich wouldn’t touch it, [Evgeny] Kissin delayed learning it, and even Prokofiev as virtuoso had got into a terrible mess trying to perform it with Ansermet and the BBC Symphony Orchestra in the 1930s, when it had gone out of his fingers."

When the composer debuted the concerto on September 5, 1913, the audience was torn between acclaim and bewilderment. In his own recounting of the premiere, the Russian composer recalled the mixed reactions of “cries of approval and boos” from the audience. “I was pleased that the concerto provoked such strong feelings in the audience,” he wrote. But soon after its debut, the piece was lost in a fire during the Russian Revolution, and he set out to write it all over again.

Premiering his newly rewritten version in 1924, the reception was once again mixed. Critics described the piece as a “cacophony of sounds that has nothing in common with civilized music,” and a “Babel of insane sounds heaped upon each other for no rhyme or reason.”

The piece was then largely forgotten for the next forty years before the Russian pianist Vladimir Ashkenazy played it with the New York Philharmonic in 1958. Since then, it has been performed hundreds of times by the world’s most talented musicians and is widely regarded as one of the most important pieces to come out of the Romantic Era.

Reading those — admittedly hilarious — reviews made me wonder what even inspired them to be written. So I took a closer look at the music itself.

For this analysis, I chose to use Kissin's 2009 recording— whose work I have always gravitated towards — with the Philharmonia Orchestra conducted by Ashkenazy, which won a Grammy that year for Best Instrumental Soloist(s) Performance (with Orchestra).

The first movement sets the tone for the rest of the concerto. The piece opens subtly, with a subdued piano tone that gradually crescendos to the first crucial part of the piece: the introduction of the first theme.



The phrase is simple: thirteen notes, a rising G minor scale with an A-flat twist at the end. It is these notes that set the foundation for the rest of the movement.

After the first movement decrescendos out, the concerto immediately picks up again in the second movement. The piano is off to the races in a perpetual motion, playing rapid sixteenth notes that force the listener forward.



Juxtaposed to the piano are short and sharp exclamations from the orchestra, adding to the building tension.



A darkly ironic, almost cartoon-like atmosphere sets in. In a short period of only two and a half minutes, the listener is left restless.

Still unsettled, the third movement arrives. No longer with the frantic energy of the second movement, the movement opens with a heavy march; Kissin joins in with brittle and cold melodies; dissonant chords clang in the upper register as the orchestra continues its terrifying march.



Near the end, the melody comes alive and overwhelms the listener, as the horns continue to blare and flutes play chilling high notes. The piano then eventually leads the charge into a decrescendo.

It all comes to a head in the fourth and final movement, where, after a frantic introduction befitting the tempo marking — allegro tempestoso — a new theme is introduced.



It’s a unique compositional choice: When one thinks of the typical format of a concerto — like those of Mozart, Tchaikovsky, or Brahms — the theme from the first movement is usually revisited in the last one. But instead, Prokofiev allows the piece to evolve beyond that. It’s a more subdued and mellow line, compared to the frantic atonal moments and the ominous marches that came before it. The biographer Daniel Jaffé calls the theme a “lullaby,” seeming to gradually lay the piece to rest. Perhaps it resembles something like moving on, acceptance.

The piano introduces this lullaby first (in a section that reminds me especially of the cadenza in Beethoven’s Piano Concerto No. 4), then the orchestra joins in, the theme echoing around the hall, swelling then disappearing into a final rendition by the bassoon.

Then another piano solo begins — Jaffe describes it as “not so much a cadenza... but a post-cadential meditation”: introspective and somber, not without its powerful moments, but more wandering than brash as in the first movement.

I find myself constantly coming back to Sergei Prokofiev’s Piano Concerto No. 2. The chaos and dissonance of the piece feel like a perfect embodiment of the inner turmoil of the composer as he grapples with the personal loss of his close friend and the political upheaval of the Russian Revolution. It feels as though I can hear Prokofiev processing his grief in real time as he writes each note.

Prokofiev’s use of modernist techniques, which remain unsettling to this day, creates one of the most captivating auditory stories I have ever listened to. Romantic compositions are often associated with warmth and lush melodies — Rachmaninoff is known for his elegance and depth; Ravel for his richness and radiance — but Prokofiev makes a starkly different stylistic choice that had initially made me hesitant to approach his work. But, knowing now Prokofiev’s reasons for writing the concerto, it is clear to me there is no other way he could have written such feelings down other than to defy the norms of what sounds “good.”

In his book, “The Concerto: A Listener’s Guide,” the author Michael Steinberg describes an article written by an old Russian newspaper reviewing the premiere of the concerto. As the performance went on, excitement turned to anticipation, then to anger. But despite the audience’s constant taunting, Prokofiev bowed defiantly — and played an encore. ■
