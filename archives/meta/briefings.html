ANALYSIS: Fueled by high energy bills, U.K. inflation continues unmitigated spiral

TORIES ARE SILENT AS COST-OF-LIVING HIKES RALLY WORKERS TO STRIKE, EXPERTS WARN OF RECESSION

PHOTO: JUSTIN TALLIS/AFP/GETTY

Citi projects U.K. inflation will breach 18% by next year.

In a research note to clients, economists for the U.S. bank set its projections for the consumer price index and retail price index in the first quarter of 2023 as 18% and 21%, respectively. This follows a similar note from the bank, released to clients Jun. 22, predicting that food price inflation would hit 20% by the first quarter of next year. The Bank of England is expected to hike interest rates ...even further in response, despite an earlier increase in borrowing costs Aug. 4 to 1.75%, its largest interest rate increase in 27 years.

The Bank also warned of a potential recession within this year, as forecasts showed the economy will continue to shrink amid still-growing inflation. Experts have pinpointed continually high energy bills, as a result of Russia's invasion of Ukraine, for the low growth.

Thousands of workers across Britain's industrial sector are on strike or threatening to strike.

Criminal barristers are just the latest to join ongoing walkouts organized by industrial workers demanding its employers and the Government for higher, livable wages, as the inflation-driven cost-of-living crisis in the U.K. remains largely unaddressed. This comes after U.K. rail transport was brought to a near-standstill last week as more than 45,000 rail workers striked over better pay and working conditions. Last month, 40,000 workers at the British telecommunications company BT and 115,000 postal workers for the Royal Mail were among those who voted to strike for similar demands.

Calls for the Conservative government to act on inflation and cost of living remain unanswered.

As the Sep. 5 date to choose Britain's next prime minister and Conservative leader looms near, critics say existing Tories in Parliament have largely been missing-in-action with regards to addressing the current economic crisis affecting the country. Liz Truss, whom polls suggest will most likely win her race against Rishi Sunak, has also offered little consolation on the matter. Truss was the subject of significant controversy last Tuesday after leaked comments (obtained by the Guardian) showed her as saying British workers needed "more graft" and had issues in "mindset and attitude" which undermined productivity. Conservatives also lost footing in polls as both Truss and Sunak promised tax cuts, which experts have argued are ineffective and unrealistic.
