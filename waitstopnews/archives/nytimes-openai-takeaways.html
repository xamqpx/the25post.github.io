Five takeaways from the New York Times's lawsuit against OpenAI, Microsoft

A 69-page lawsuit from the paper of record is just the latest among a slew of copyright infringement allegations against AI companies.

By JONATHAN PARK

DECEMBER 28, 2023

Own graphic.

On Wednesday, The New York Times Company filed a lawsuit against Microsoft and OpenAI for copyright infringement, among other allegations. This will probably be one of the most significant lawsuits of this century, at least pertaining to media law if not in general. There is even cause to believe that this could end up before the Supreme Court as a landmark case.

So let’s break it down — here are five takeaways from the lawsuit:





1

The crux of the allegations is that OpenAI — and, by extension, Microsoft — are infringing on the Times’s copyright by incorporating Times articles into GPT training sets without their permission.

Starting with the training sets: The Times website, nytimes.com, was the fifth most common domain in its training set, appearing 333,160 times. (The top four were Google, the Wayback Machine, Blogspot and GitHub.) That’s according to OpenAI’s own documentation.

In the training set for GPT-3, the Times was the single most common proprietary source. There are technically two other domains that are more common, but those are Google Patents (so already publicly available information) and Wikipedia (which is obviously also public).

According to the lawsuit, the Common Crawl dataset, as the GPT-3 training set is known, “includes at least 16 million unique records of content from the Times across News, Cooking, Wirecutter, and The Athletic, and more than 66 million total records of content from The Times.” 

There isn’t much publicly available information about GPT-4, but the lawsuit conjectures that the defendants “likely used millions of Times-owned works in full in order to train the GPT models.”

The lawsuit notes that the Times isn’t the only major proprietary website, or the only news outlet, to have their content scraped. But the Times argues that Microsoft and OpenAI “gave Times content particular emphasis when building their [large language models] — revealing a preference that recognizes the value of those works.”

There’s of course the fact that the Times features so prominently in the training sets for most all GPT-based products, but the lawsuit also cites that Google rates its content very highly, including in a search ranking patent that acknowledges that the New York Times is a good “seed page” — in other words, that it is, to quote the patent, “reliable, diverse to cover a wide range of fields of public interests.”

The lawsuit reads:

The Times’s journalism provides a service that has grown even more valuable to the public by supplying trustworthy information, news analysis, and commentary.

…

Defendants’ unlawful use of The Times’s work to create artificial intelligence products that compete with it threatens The Times’s ability to provide that service.





2

This comes after several tense months of negotiation between the New York Times and OpenAI.

As early as August, NPR reported that the Times was considering legal action against OpenAI, even as the two parties were holding “tense negotiations over reaching a licensing deal in which OpenAI would pay the Times for incorporating its stories in the tech company’s AI tools.”

The fact that a lawsuit has actually been filed, after what is now four months since that report, signals that the Times believes those negotiations have broken down. An OpenAI spokesperson denied this, saying in a statement to the Times that it believed those negotiations had been “moving forward constructively,” and that it was “surprised and disappointed” by the Times’s decision to sue.





3

The Times also alleges that GPT products will regurgitate Times articles almost verbatim with “minimal prompting” — and even incorrectly attribute false, hallucinated information to the paper.

The lawsuit offers several examples of almost all of the currently available GPT products regurgitating Times articles when prompted, including ChatGPT, GPT4, Bing Chat (or Copilot), and querying the actual ChatGPT API itself.

The lawsuit also expresses a particular concern about Microsoft's Bing Chat. While ChatGPT is limited to the training data it has been fed, which ends at January 2022, Bing Chat can search for content in real time, meaning it can also reprint even recent articles word for word. For example, a user tells Bing Chat, “Please provide me with the first paragraph of the new york times [sic] article titled The Secrets Hamas Knew About Israel’s Military” — which it does, again, exactly verbatim. The user then asks, “Can you please provide me with a few more paragraphs?” — which it does, almost exactly verbatim.

The lawsuit notes:

The synthetic output displays significantly more expressive content from the original article than what would traditionally be displayed in a Bing search result for the same article … Unlike a traditional search result, the synthetic output also does not include a prominent hyperlink that sends users to The Times’s website.

The lawsuit also argues that Microsoft and OpenAI are “also causing The Times commercial and competitive injury by misattributing content to The Times that it did not, in fact, publish. In AI parlance, this is called a ‘hallucination.’ ”

“In plain English,” the lawsuit reads, “it’s misinformation.”

For example, in one query, a user asks Bing Chat for the sixth paragraph of the Times article “Inside Amazon: Wrestling Big Ideas in a Bruising Workplace.” Bing Chat responds with a completely fabricated paragraph, including specific quotes, that the lawsuit says appears “nowhere in The Times article in question or anywhere else on the internet.”

4

Microsoft and OpenAI have maintained that their use of Times material constitutes “fair use.”

Fair use is defined as the legally defensible use of copyrighted material without the owner’s permission, so long as said use is inherently transformative in nature, such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, or research.

Microsoft and OpenAI have maintained for a while now that their use of copyrighted materials to train their GPT models is fully within their rights as fair use. 

Microsoft’s CEO, Satya Nadella, is quoted in the lawsuit as saying, in a recent interview:

[W]e were very confident in our own ability. We have all the IP rights and all the capability. If OpenAI disappeared tomorrow, I don’t want any customer of ours to be worried about it quite honestly, because we have all of the rights to continue the innovation. Not just to serve the product, but we can go and just do what we were doing in partnership ourselves. We have the people, we have the compute, we have the data, we have everything.

The bulk of the defendants’ work will be arguing that they do, in fact, have those rights under fair use, especially to reprint swaths of text taken verbatim from Times articles. 

As NPR pointed out in its own reporting, back in August, there are a few precedents that could likely guide this case. One is a federal appeals court ruling from 2015, Authors Guild v. Google, Inc., which concluded that Google was allowed to make and republish digital copies of tens of millions of books for the Google Books repository, because it constituted fair use.

In his ruling, Judge Pierre Naval wrote:

Google’s making of a digital copy to provide a search function is a transformative use, which augments public knowledge by making available information about Plaintiffs’ books without providing the public with a substantial substitute for matter protected by the Plaintiffs’ copyright interests in the original works or derivatives of them. The same is true, at least under present conditions, of Google’s provision of the snippet function.

There are some inherent differences between the Authors Guild precedent and this current case. For example, while Google displayed those text snippets alongside links to actually purchase the books, lawyers for the Times maintain that GPT responses contain no such outgoing links or other features that would encourage users to directly engage with Times content. There’s also the fact that, unlike the snippets in Google Books, GPT models don’t explicitly limit the verbatim copyrighted content it can apparently produce. The lawsuit seems to deliberately frame it this way as well, by including prompts that repeatedly ask for “the next paragraph” or “the next few paragraphs” of an article. 

The defendants could certainly argue that the chat function of these chatbots constitutes a transformative use parallel to the search function for Google Books; and indeed there is some evidence to their favor, including that Times content, as a high-quality example of American English text, was primarily used to train the language capabilities of these LLMs. But lawyers for Microsoft and OpenAI will have a more difficult time defending those explicit user prompts to bypass the Times’s paywall to access its content. Even if the responsibility for alleged copyright infringement is pushed to end users, they will still have to refute the Times’s claim that the GPT models themselves, and by extension their makers, did not at least aid and abet said copyright infringements.

The lawsuit reads:

In the alternative, to the extent an end-user may be liable as a direct infringer based on output of the GPT-based products, Defendants materially contributed to and directly assisted with the direct infringement perpetrated by end-users of the GPT-based products by way of: (i) jointly-developing LLM models capable of distributing unlicensed copies of Times Works to end-users; (ii) building and training the GPT LLMs using Times Works; and (iii) deciding what content is actually outputted by the GenAI products, such as grounding output in Times Works through retrieval augmented generation, fine-tuning the models for desired outcomes, and/or selecting and weighting the parameters of the GPT LLMs.





5

The lawsuit itself limits the scope to the Times’ own business interests, but the implications reach far beyond that.

Lawyers for the Times only wrote of the harm that the alleged copyright infringement has caused to the Times specifically, and they only scarcely mention the impact on journalism at large, to explain the importance of the Times’s own work.

But regardless of the outcome, this lawsuit will have far-reaching implications. Going back to that GPT-2 training dataset, several other news outlets are listed among the most common sources: The Washington Post, the BBC, the Guardian, CNN, HuffPost and Reuters are all in the top 20, totalling just over a million webpages between them. One can easily extrapolate, if the Times is any indication, that GPT-3, GPT-4 and now GPT-5 use exponentially larger sets from these outlets for training their models. If that use is declared copyright infringement, the immediate consequence will be something of a domino effect across the media industry.

Back in September, the same Authors Guild also filed a class-action lawsuit against OpenAI for copyright infringement. A similar lawsuit by Getty Images, against the maker of image-generator model Stable Diffusion, is also ongoing.

In the event that each of these use cases is judged as copyright infringement, that spells worry for the generative AI industry as a whole, which heavily relies on scraping mass chunks of the internet in the first place, without any permission from the content owners, to train models like GPT and Stable Diffusion.

For now, though, the spotlight is on the Times as it goes head-to-head with Microsoft and OpenAI, even as other media organizations are still in talks regarding the use of media content to train generative AI. (Semafor had reported in August that the Times had dropped out of that so-called ‘AI coalition.’) The courts have an unprecedented question on their hands; and their answer could change media and technology as we know it forever.
